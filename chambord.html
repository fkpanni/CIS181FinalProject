A Renaissance Masterpiece

Château de Chambord is the largest château in the Loire Valley and one of the most recognizable in the world due to its very distinct French Renaissance architecture that blends traditional French medieval forms with classical Italian structures. Built as a hunting lodge for King Francis I, it features 440 rooms, 365 fireplaces, and 84 staircases.

The most notable architectural feature is the spectacular double helix staircase that is rumored to have been designed by Leonardo da Vinci. The two helices ascend the three floors without ever meeting, illuminated from above by a sort of lighthouse at the highest point of the château.

Through the Centuries

  • 1519: Construction begins under Francis I
  • 1547: Francis I dies with château incomplete
  • 1639: Louis XIII gives it to his brother Gaston d'Orléans
  • 1682: Louis XIV completes major additions
  • 1725: Becomes home to exiled Polish king Stanislas Leszczyński
  • 1840: Listed as a historic monument
  • 1930: French state acquires full ownership
  • 1981: Designated UNESCO World Heritage Site

Architectural Highlights

  • Double Helix Staircase: Possibly designed by Leonardo da Vinci
  • French Medieval Forms: Keep, corner towers, and moat
  • Italian Renaissance: Symmetrical layout and classical motifs
  • Rooftop Terrace: Offers panoramic views of the estate
